# About Me

I'm **Alex**, and I care about making robots and helping others by making robotics easier to learn!

## What I do

I'm a mechanical engineer who specializes in assitive robotics (wheelchairs) and advanced motor controls.  I studied mechanical engineering, and then started working in R&D in the assitive tech space.  When I learned how to make PCB's on my own, I got addicted to open sourcing them.  EVERY project I make, you can download the kicad files directly and you can also view them on Kicanvas!  A lot of them are linked here in the project cards of the website :)

## What I want to do

In short, I want to develop Open Source Hardware PCBs and Robotics solutions that move the robotics industry forward and absolutely ruin a bunch of patents by providing free and transparent solutions to the "moats" created and upheld by a plethora of anti-innovation industries. 

## Philosophy
My philosophy is built around my upbringing in the rural deep southern US.  The education system down south is spuradic, leaving a lot of the population in a disadvantaged position when trying to grow beyond what their small town can offer.  This is slowly being changed by the internet, though many places in the south are only just now reaching acceptable speeds of internet.

### Where do robots come in? Robotics as a means of change
Robots allow us to multiply our efforts.  Many have a misconception that robots can only be used by companies and factories for bigger profits - but if the public had the knowledge it could also be used by our local communities FOR our local communities. 

I think people innately find happiness in changing their environment and community for the better.  

Through robotics, there is a world where community gardens could be automated, insulin could be free and sourced in your neighborhood from an autonomous bacterial culture, clothes could be made for the poor, and food made for the hungry.  These could exist not as a corporate service you subscribe to, but as a community robot system that you pitch in to maintain!

## The Tricky Part:  How to start?
I would say you start by building robots to fix problems you see - but most people don't know how!  Whats worse, is the educational path through robotics involves many "restarts" and product ecosystems.  This comes from most educational products being either too niche, too dumbed down, too lacking in educational fundamentals, or some combination of the mix.  The solution is not to make education-specific products, or at least not at the cost of the function!  Like how crayons in school can still draw, and how your first frying pan can still fry an egg.  

### A product won't solve this. To do this we need to know how to develop robots together and for ourselves. 
Instead, we need to teach each other and build our educational tools "ourselves", or for our communities.  The ideal robotics ecosystem doesn't have "dumbed down" lines drawn in the sand. The learning path for a user shouldn't go over learning-curve cliffs as they transfer from intro to intermediate to advanced.  If we change this strategy, we can train not only roboticists, but a culture of Free Open Source contribution :)

**Education should be built into the ecosystem** so that everyone can participate in building and learning together.

Growing up I always felt like I was fighting for educational resources, and it infuriated me that location and money was holding me back from learning about the world.  Now the internet has provided the ability to democratize education - but that doesn't mean it is democratized!  **WE have to put in the WORK to democratize it**

## The work I'm doing:
I've built many motor controllers, PCB projects, educational demos, and platforms to grow and sharpen this philosophy — from tiny Kickstarter boards to research tools used at Carnegie Mellon for robotics education, and I help my roboticist friends with their projects when they need hardware support and custom designs because they are a prime user case of people in need of more advanced features!!!
